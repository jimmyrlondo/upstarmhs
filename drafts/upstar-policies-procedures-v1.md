## Section 1: ASPIRE to Excellence — Organizational Leadership & Business Practices

This section establishes policies that apply organization-wide at Upstar Mental Health Services, regardless of program or service type, in conformance with Section 1 (ASPIRE to Excellence) of the CARF 2026 Behavioral Health Standards Manual.

### Policy 1.A — Leadership Structure and Accountability

#### Policy Statement
Upstar Mental Health Services maintains a clearly defined leadership structure with documented roles, responsibilities, and lines of authority. Leadership is accountable for the organization's mission, ethical conduct, corporate compliance, resource stewardship, performance improvement, and the person-centered direction of its programs and services.

**CARF Reference:** Standards 1.A.1–1.A.9 (2026 Behavioral Health Standards Manual, Section 1.A, Leadership), including corporate responsibility (1.A.6), U.S. corporate-compliance obligations (1.A.7), leadership education/resources (1.A.8), and accountability for performance and quality (1.A.9).

#### Procedure

##### 1. Leadership Structure
- The CEO maintains a written organizational chart identifying each leadership position, its title, and its lines of reporting authority.
- The CEO shall update the organizational chart whenever a leadership role, title, or reporting line changes, and ensure the current version is accessible to all staff within 7 calendar days of any change.
- Each leadership position shall have a written job description specifying its responsibilities, maintained by `[Placeholder — Human Resources / the CEO; confirm custodian]`.
- The organization identifies, at minimum, the following functions and the position(s) accountable for each: clinical oversight (Clinical Director), financial management (CEO/Finance), workforce/HR, privacy/confidentiality, health and safety, risk management, and compliance/ethics.

##### 2. Person-Centered Philosophy
- Leadership defines and documents Upstar's person-centered philosophy in writing, addressing individuality, autonomy, respect for diversity, cultural competence, empowerment, partnership with persons served, and trauma-informed practice.
- The philosophy shall be communicated to stakeholders through `[Placeholder — confirm channels, e.g., new-staff orientation, persons-served handbook, website, posted signage]`.
- All staff and contractors shall receive training on the person-centered philosophy at orientation and review it at least annually.

##### 3. Scope of Leadership Responsibility
Leadership guides and is accountable for the following organizational functions:
- Establishing and communicating the mission and strategic direction (see Policy 1.C).
- Promoting the value and achievement of outcomes of programs and services (see Policies 1.L–1.M).
- Balancing the expectations of persons served against other stakeholder needs.
- Maintaining financial solvency (see Policy 1.F).
- Risk management (see Policy 1.G) and health and safety (see Policy 1.H).
- Corporate responsibility, ethics, and legal/regulatory compliance (see Sections 6–7 below and Policy 1.E).
- Succession planning (see Section 5 below and Policy 1.I).
- Technology and data governance, including any future use of artificial/augmented intelligence (see Section 7 below).
- Ongoing performance improvement and at least annual review of all organizational policies (see Section 4 below).

##### 4. Policy Review Cycle
- The CEO, together with the leadership team, shall review all organizational policies at least annually, and on an event-triggered interim basis following any regulatory change, critical incident, adverse audit finding, material service change, or identified policy gap.
- Each review evaluates every policy for continued relevance, legal/regulatory currency, and alignment with current operations, and identifies policies requiring revision or retirement.
- Leadership documents the date of each review and any resulting revisions in the **Policy Review Log** maintained with this manual.
- Program-specific policies may also be reviewed by that program's leadership, with proposed revisions forwarded to the CEO for final approval. The CEO is the sole approval authority for this manual unless governance documents assign that authority to a board.

##### 5. Succession Planning
- Leadership maintains a written succession plan identifying actions to be taken if a key leadership position becomes vacant due to retirement, resignation, illness, death, or other cause.
- The plan identifies, where feasible, internal staff who could assume key roles on an interim or permanent basis, and notes roles requiring external recruitment.
- The succession plan is reviewed at least annually (and whenever leadership composition changes) as part of the review cycle in Section 4. See Policy 1.I, Section 8 for workforce-level succession planning.

##### 6. Corporate Responsibility, Compliance, and Ethical Conduct
- Upstar maintains a written **Code of Ethical Conduct** addressing, at minimum: business practices and marketing; contractual relationships and conflicts of interest; use of social media; service-delivery boundaries; protection of confidential information; fiscal integrity; and procedures for reporting suspected violations.
- **Corporate compliance (U.S. — 1.A.7):** Upstar implements a corporate-compliance approach covering applicable laws and regulations governing billing, coding, documentation, and reporting; mechanisms for prevention, detection, and response; and staff education on those obligations.
- **Reporting and investigation:** Staff and stakeholders may report suspected violations to `[Placeholder — designated Ethics/Compliance contact; see Open Questions #13]` without fear of reprisal. Reports are documented, reviewed, and investigated within defined timeframes.
- All staff, contractors, and applicable other stakeholders (e.g., board members, volunteers) receive education on the Code of Ethical Conduct at orientation and at least annually.

##### 7. Technology Governance and Use of Artificial/Augmented Intelligence (AI)
- **Status: Not currently applicable.** As of the effective date, Upstar does not use AI tools in service delivery, clinical documentation, or data analysis. Standard 1.A.5 requires a written AI policy only if AI is used.
- Should Upstar adopt any AI tool, this section must be completed and approved by the CEO **before** implementation, addressing: (a) how AI is used; (b) disclosure to persons served; (c) protections for privacy, confidentiality, and data accuracy; (d) human review and accountability; and (e) monitoring of risks and outcomes.

##### 8. Leadership Resources, Education, and Accountability
- Leadership provides resources and education enabling staff to understand and apply this manual, including orientation and annual refreshers (1.A.8).
- To demonstrate accountability (1.A.9), the CEO ensures performance information (Policies 1.L–1.M), financial results (Policy 1.F), and risk/safety findings (Policies 1.G–1.H) are reviewed at least quarterly with documented follow-up.

#### Required Documentation
- Current organizational chart; leadership job descriptions; written person-centered philosophy; Policy Review Log; written succession plan; Code of Ethical Conduct and corporate-compliance plan; annual ethics/compliance training records; quarterly leadership review records.

#### Monitoring / KPIs
- 100% of staff/contractors complete person-centered philosophy and ethics training at orientation and annually.
- Organizational chart updated within 7 calendar days of any leadership change.
- All policies reviewed at least annually (0 policies overdue).
- 100% of ethics/compliance reports receive an initial determination within the defined business-day window.

#### Responsible Party
CEO, in conjunction with the leadership team (Clinical Director and designees as noted).

#### Items Requiring Organization Input
1. Communication channels for the person-centered philosophy; 2. Custodian of job descriptions; 3. Quarterly review target months; 4. Designated Ethics/Compliance contact and reporting timeframes.

### Policy 1.B — Governance

> **Applicability note:** CARF Section 1.B (Governance) applies to organizations with a formal governing board and is optional otherwise. This policy is included for completeness. Upstar shall complete the "applicability" determination before final adoption.

#### Policy Statement
Where Upstar maintains a governing board, the board implements written governance policies addressing its structure, authority, fiduciary duties, conflicts of interest, executive selection, executive evaluation, and accountability for organizational mission and compliance.

**CARF Reference:** Standards 1.B.1–1.B.7 (2026 Behavioral Health Standards Manual, Section 1.B, Governance — optional).

#### Procedure

##### 1. Governance Framework
- The board implements written governance policies defining board structure, size, terms, officer roles, and decision-making authority versus authority delegated to the CEO.

##### 2. Governance Policy Content
- Governance policies address, at minimum: board member roles and responsibilities; fiduciary and legal duties; conflict-of-interest disclosure and management; orientation and ongoing education of board members; succession/continuity; and committee structure, if used.

##### 3. Board–Executive Relationship
- Governance policies define the board's relationship with executive leadership, including delegation of operational authority to the CEO and the boundaries of board involvement in operations.

##### 4. Board Processes
- Board processes include scheduled meetings, recorded minutes, quorum requirements, and documented decisions.

##### 5. Executive Selection and Succession
- Governance policies address executive selection and succession for the CEO position, coordinated with Policy 1.A, Section 5.

##### 6. Executive Evaluation and Compensation
- Governance policies address executive performance evaluation and compensation review on a defined cycle (at least annually), documented in board records.

##### 7. Governance Self-Review
- The board reviews its own governance practices at least annually and documents resulting improvements.

#### Required Documentation
- Board bylaws/governance policies; conflict-of-interest disclosures; board meeting minutes; executive evaluation and compensation records; annual governance self-review.

#### Monitoring / KPIs
- Annual board self-review completed; annual CEO evaluation completed; 100% of board members with current conflict-of-interest disclosures on file.

#### Responsible Party
Governing board (if any); otherwise CEO documents Not-Applicable status.

#### Items Requiring Organization Input
1. Confirm whether Upstar has a formal governing board or operates leadership-only; 2. If board exists, attach bylaws and conflict-of-interest policy; 3. If no board, document Not-Applicable determination.

### Policy 1.C — Strategic Planning

#### Policy Statement
Upstar engages in ongoing strategic planning that accounts for the expectations of persons served and other stakeholders; the competitive and regulatory environment; financial opportunities and threats; organizational capabilities; social determinants of health; and the use of technology and data to support access, efficiency, and outcomes.

**CARF Reference:** Standards 1.C.1–1.C.3 (2026 Behavioral Health Standards Manual, Section 1.C, Strategic Planning).

#### Procedure

##### 1. Establishing and Maintaining the Strategic Plan
- **Status: Initial plan in development.** Upstar does not yet have a written strategic plan. The CEO shall lead development of the first written strategic plan, to be completed by `[Placeholder — target date]`.
- The CEO leads an annual strategic planning cycle that begins each `[Placeholder — month, e.g., September]` so the updated plan is adopted before the start of the following fiscal/program year.
- Planning gathers documented input from persons served, staff, and other stakeholders through the mechanisms in Policy 1.D.

##### 2. Environmental and Capability Analysis
As part of each cycle, leadership reviews and documents Upstar's position on each factor:
- Expectations of persons served and other stakeholders.
- The competitive environment and the regulatory/legislative environment, including relevant funding or policy changes.
- Financial opportunities and threats.
- Organizational capabilities, including staffing and succession needs in key administrative, financial, and service-delivery positions.
- Social determinants of health affecting persons served (e.g., transportation, housing, food security, healthcare access, exposure to trauma or violence).
- Service-area demographics and how they are changing; relationships with external stakeholders.
- Current and future use of technology and data, including gaps, and how technology will support efficient business processes, effective service delivery, increased access, and performance improvement.
- Findings from the most recent performance analysis (Policy 1.L).

##### 3. Plan Content, Approval, and Sharing
- The written plan identifies organizational priorities, goals, and measurable objectives for the planning period, and assigns responsibility for each.
- The CEO approves the plan, maintains the current version on file, and ensures it is accessible to leadership and, in summary form, to staff and (as relevant) other stakeholders (1.C.3).

##### 4. Annual Review and Update
- The plan is formally reviewed and updated at least annually, documenting progress against prior goals and revising priorities based on updated input and environmental factors. The date and outcome of the review are documented.

#### Required Documentation
- Written strategic plan; environmental/capability analysis; documented stakeholder input; annual review notes; Policy Review Log entries.

#### Monitoring / KPIs
- Strategic plan reviewed/updated at least annually (on schedule).
- ≥ `[Placeholder — target %]` of strategic objectives on track or completed at annual review.
- Documented input obtained from all three stakeholder groups (persons served, staff, other stakeholders) each cycle.

#### Responsible Party
CEO, in conjunction with the leadership team.

#### Items Requiring Organization Input

### Policy 1.F — Financial Planning and Management

#### Policy Statement
Upstar conducts fiscal management designed to support its mission, outcomes for persons served, and organizational performance objectives. Upstar prepares an annual budget prior to the start of each fiscal year, monitors results against budget, maintains fiscal policies and internal controls, reviews billing accuracy, maintains current fee schedules, and obtains an independent annual financial review or audit.

**CARF Reference:** Standards 1.F.1–1.F.6 (2026 Behavioral Health Standards Manual, Section 1.F, Financial Planning and Management).

#### Procedure

##### 1. Annual Budget Development
- **Status: Formal budget process to be established.** The CEO shall establish a formal written annual budget process beginning with the `[Placeholder — upcoming fiscal year]` budget.
- The CEO prepares a written annual budget prior to the start of each fiscal year (fiscal year = `[Placeholder — e.g., January 1–December 31]`).
- The budget reflects input from staff and stakeholders as appropriate; comparison to the prior year's actuals; anticipated cash-flow needs; and relevant external factors (e.g., reimbursement-rate changes, program growth, staffing changes).
- The budget documents reasonable projections of revenues, expenses, and capital expenditures, and is approved by the CEO before the fiscal year begins. A provisional budget may be adopted pending final figures if needed.
- The approved budget is shared, as appropriate, with staff and other stakeholders.

##### 2. Monitoring Financial Performance
- The CEO reviews actual results against budget at least monthly, identifying and documenting revenues/expenses; internal trends, challenges, opportunities, and management information (e.g., billing lag, denials, collections).
- Where improvement is needed, the CEO documents and implements corrective actions.
- Financial results are reported, as appropriate, to staff and stakeholders. Overall financial solvency is reviewed at least annually by leadership, with a remediation plan developed if solvency concerns are identified.

##### 3. Fiscal Policies and Internal Controls
- Upstar implements written fiscal policies and internal controls establishing responsibility and authority for: purchasing, writing checks, making deposits, petty cash, credit/debit card use, billing, cash handling, payroll, and account reconciliation.
- `[Placeholder — document the specific internal control structure: e.g., dual signatures for checks above a set dollar amount, separation of duties between billing and cash handling, check-signing authority, approval thresholds.]`
- All staff with fiscal responsibilities receive initial training at hire/assignment and ongoing training whenever policies are revised.

##### 4. Billing Accuracy Review (Mandatory — third-party billing)

- At least quarterly, a trained individual (`[Placeholder — reviewer role; see Open Questions #9]`) reviews a representative sample of bills, comparing dates, units, and types of services billed to supporting documentation and payer rules.

##### 5. Fee Structures

- The CEO retains the results, including recommendations, and documents management's response, including corrective actions or rationale where none are taken.

- Annual independent review/audit completed; all recommendations addressed or rationale documented.

#### Responsible Party
CEO (with bookkeeper/accountant and independent CPA as noted).

#### Items Requiring Organization Input

### Policy 2.F — Promoting Nonviolent Practices (Crisis Prevention & Least Restrictive Intervention)

> **Applicability note:** Section 2.F applies only if Upstar uses seclusion and/or restraint. If Upstar does not use either intervention, the CEO and Clinical Director shall document a not-applicable determination and retain the determination with this manual.

#### Policy Statement
Upstar promotes nonviolent practices, trauma-informed de-escalation, and the least restrictive interventions necessary to protect immediate safety. Seclusion and restraint, if ever used, are treated as last-resort, time-limited emergency measures subject to strict authorization, monitoring, documentation, review, and reduction planning.

**CARF Reference:** Standards 2.F.1–2.F.11 (2026 Behavioral Health Standards Manual, Section 2.F, Promoting Nonviolent Practices).

#### Procedure

##### 1. Governing Principles and Prohibited Uses (2.F.1)
- Written policy specifies that seclusion or restraint may be used only to address an imminent risk of serious harm after less restrictive interventions have been attempted or determined clinically unsafe or ineffective.
- The policy prohibits use of seclusion or restraint for discipline, staff convenience, coercion, retaliation, or as a substitute for active treatment, staffing, or environmental modification.
- Upstar documents the interventions it recognizes (e.g., verbal de-escalation, environmental change, sensory modulation, voluntary time-out, enhanced observation) and defines the circumstances in which each may be used.

##### 2. Workforce Training and Competency (2.F.2)
- All personnel who might participate in crisis response receive initial and annual competency-based training in trauma-informed de-escalation, behavior support, recognition of escalating risk, safe intervention techniques approved by the organization, monitoring obligations, first aid, and current CPR.
- Training records identify the curriculum used, instructor, date completed, demonstrated competencies, and due date for retraining.
- Only personnel with current documented competency may participate in any restrictive intervention.

##### 3. Elimination and Reduction Plan (2.F.3)
- The Clinical Director maintains a written plan to reduce and, where feasible, eliminate the use of seclusion and restraint through staff training, individualized prevention planning, environmental supports, post-event learning, and leadership oversight.
- The plan is reviewed at least annually and whenever trend data, sentinel events, or debriefing findings indicate additional controls are needed.

##### 4. Authorization and Implementation Controls (2.F.4–2.F.7)
- Written procedures govern who may authorize seclusion or restraint, what assessments are required before and during use, how alternatives are documented, and how the intervention is ended at the earliest safe opportunity.
- When seclusion or restraint is used, staff document the precipitating event, behaviors creating imminent risk, alternatives attempted, intervention used, start/stop time, monitoring observations, injuries if any, and persons notified.
- Orders for restraint or seclusion, if required by the service setting or payer rules, shall be obtained, renewed, and time-limited in accordance with applicable law and program licensure requirements. `[OHIO LEGAL REVIEW REQUIRED]`

##### 5. Monitoring During Use and Physical Environment (2.F.5–2.F.8)
- The person served is monitored continuously or at the frequency required by law, licensure, or program design, with attention to circulation, breathing, distress, medical risk factors, and psychological status.
- If a designated seclusion room exists, the room must protect privacy and dignity while allowing safe observation, rapid access by staff, and prompt emergency response.
- Medical consultation is obtained immediately if injury, respiratory compromise, loss of consciousness, medication concern, or other significant clinical risk is observed.

##### 6. Notifications, Debriefing, and Leadership Review (2.F.9–2.F.10)
- Following each event, Upstar completes a documented debriefing with involved staff and, when clinically appropriate, with the person served and family/support system consistent with the person’s preferences and legal authority.
- Debriefing addresses precipitating factors, opportunities to revise the person’s safety plan, trauma impact, staffing/environmental issues, and actions needed to prevent recurrence.
- The Clinical Director or designee completes a timely leadership review of each event and escalates serious events through the incident-review process in Policy 1.H.

##### 7. Annual Analysis and Performance Improvement (2.F.11)
- At least annually, Upstar analyzes the use of seclusion and restraint by frequency, duration, time of day, location, staff involved, injuries, precipitating factors, and persons served impacted.
- Findings are used to update training, staffing, program design, and the elimination/reduction plan, and are incorporated into performance-improvement activities under Policy 1.M.

#### Required Documentation
- Written nonviolent-practices policy; approved intervention curriculum; staff competency and CPR/first-aid records; reduction/elimination plan; event documentation; orders where applicable; debriefing records; annual analysis and action plans.

#### Monitoring / KPIs
- 100% of involved staff maintain current crisis-intervention competency and first-aid/CPR training.
- 100% of seclusion/restraint events include same-day documentation, leadership review, and documented debriefing within the defined timeframe.
- Total number and duration of restrictive interventions trend downward over time.

#### Responsible Party
Clinical Director, with Program Director/Charge Nurse/Medical Director as applicable to the service setting.

#### Items Requiring Organization Input
1. Whether Upstar uses seclusion and/or restraint in any program; 2. Approved crisis-intervention curriculum and retraining cycle; 3. Monitoring frequency and notification timeframes by setting; 4. Whether Upstar maintains a designated seclusion room in any location.

## Appendix D — Key Definitions / Glossary

- **ACT:** Assertive Community Treatment, an intensive multidisciplinary community-based treatment model.
- **CCBHC:** Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinic.
- **DCO:** Designated Collaborating Organization used in the CCBHC model to provide required services by formal arrangement.
- **ICT / Telehealth:** Service delivery using information and communication technologies, including approved remote modalities.
- **Person served:** The individual receiving services and, when applicable, the legally authorized representative or others involved at the individual’s choice.
- **Person-centered plan:** The written plan of care/treatment that reflects the goals, strengths, preferences, and needs of the person served.
- **Restrictive intervention:** Seclusion or restraint used only as a last-resort emergency safety intervention where legally and programmatically permitted.
- **Specialty designation:** Additional CARF standards that apply when a program seeks recognition for a specialized population or service focus.
