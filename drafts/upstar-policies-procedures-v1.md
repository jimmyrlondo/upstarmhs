## Section 1: ASPIRE to Excellence — Organizational Leadership & Business Practices

This section establishes policies that apply organization-wide at Upstar Mental Health Services, regardless of program or service type, in conformance with Section 1 (ASPIRE to Excellence) of the CARF 2026 Behavioral Health Standards Manual. These policies bind all staff, contractors, leadership, volunteers, students, and peer support specialists involved in the delivery, oversight, or support of Upstar programs and services.

### Policy 1.A — Leadership Structure and Accountability

#### Policy Statement
Upstar Mental Health Services maintains a clearly defined leadership structure with documented roles, responsibilities, and lines of authority. Leadership is accountable for the organization's mission, strategic direction, financial solvency, risk management, regulatory compliance, health and safety, succession planning, corporate responsibility/ethics, technology governance, and ongoing performance improvement. Leadership maintains and communicates a person-centered philosophy that governs service delivery, and remains accessible to persons served, staff, contractors, and other stakeholders.

**CARF Reference:** Standards 1.A.1–1.A.9 (2026 Behavioral Health Standards Manual, Section 1.A, Leadership), including corporate responsibility (1.A.6), U.S. corporate-compliance obligations (1.A.7), resources and education for leadership (1.A.8), and demonstrated accountability (1.A.9). AI governance (1.A.5) is addressed in Section 7 below.

#### Procedure

##### 1. Leadership Structure
- The CEO maintains a written organizational chart identifying each leadership position, its title, and its lines of reporting authority.
- The CEO shall update the organizational chart whenever a leadership role, title, or reporting line changes, and ensure the current version is accessible to all staff within 7 calendar days of any change.
- Each leadership position shall have a written job description specifying its responsibilities, maintained by `[Placeholder — Human Resources / the CEO; confirm custodian]`.
- The organization identifies, at minimum, the following functions and the position(s) accountable for each: clinical oversight (Clinical Director), financial management (CEO/Finance), workforce/HR, health and safety (Safety Officer), privacy/records, and quality/performance improvement.

##### 2. Person-Centered Philosophy
- Leadership defines and documents Upstar's person-centered philosophy in writing, addressing individuality, autonomy, respect for diversity, cultural competence, empowerment, partnership with persons served and families/support systems, participation in valued life roles, and quality of life.
- The philosophy shall be communicated to stakeholders through `[Placeholder — confirm channels, e.g., new-staff orientation, persons-served handbook, website, posted signage]`.
- All staff and contractors shall receive training on the person-centered philosophy at orientation and review it at least annually.

##### 3. Scope of Leadership Responsibility
Leadership guides and is accountable for the following organizational functions:
- Establishing and communicating the mission and strategic direction (see Policy 1.C).
- Promoting the value and achievement of outcomes of programs and services (see Policies 1.L–1.M).
- Balancing the expectations of persons served against other stakeholder needs.
- Maintaining financial solvency (see Policy 1.F).
- Risk management (see Policy 1.G) and health and safety (see Policy 1.H).
- Corporate responsibility, ethics, and legal/regulatory compliance (see Sections 6–7 below and Policy 1.E).
- Succession planning (see Section 5 below and Policy 1.I).
- Technology and data governance, including any future use of artificial/augmented intelligence (see Section 7 below).
- Ongoing performance improvement and at least annual review of all organizational policies (see Section 4 below).

##### 4. Policy Review Cycle
- The CEO, together with the leadership team, shall review all organizational policies at least annually, and on an event-triggered interim basis following any regulatory change, critical incident trend, adverse audit finding, or material change in services. `[ENHANCEMENT]` Upstar also conducts a quarterly leadership check-in (target months `[Placeholder — e.g., January, April, July, October]`) to flag policies needing interim revision.
- Each review evaluates every policy for continued relevance, legal/regulatory currency, and alignment with current operations, and identifies policies requiring revision or retirement.
- Leadership documents the date of each review and any resulting revisions in the **Policy Review Log** maintained with this manual.
- Program-specific policies may also be reviewed by that program's leadership, with proposed revisions forwarded to the CEO for final approval. The CEO is the sole approval authority for this manual.

##### 5. Succession Planning
- Leadership maintains a written succession plan identifying actions to be taken if a key leadership position becomes vacant due to retirement, resignation, illness, death, or other cause.
- The plan identifies, where feasible, internal staff who could assume key roles on an interim or permanent basis, and notes roles requiring external recruitment.
- The succession plan is reviewed at least annually (and whenever leadership composition changes) as part of the review cycle in Section 4. See Policy 1.I, Section 8 for workforce-level succession detail.

##### 6. Corporate Responsibility, Compliance, and Ethical Conduct
- Upstar maintains a written **Code of Ethical Conduct** addressing, at minimum: business practices and marketing; contractual relationships and conflicts of interest; use of social media; service-delivery conduct (exchange of gifts/money/gratuities, personal fundraising, personal property, professional boundaries, witnessing of legal documents); professional responsibilities and HR practices; organizational fundraising (if applicable); and the prohibition of waste, fraud, abuse, and other wrongdoing.
- **Corporate compliance (U.S. — 1.A.7):** Upstar implements a corporate-compliance approach covering applicable laws and regulations governing billing, coding, documentation, and reporting; mechanisms to prevent and detect fraud, waste, and abuse; and a process for correcting and self-reporting identified violations consistent with payer and legal requirements `[OHIO LEGAL REVIEW REQUIRED — confirm Medicaid/Medicare self-disclosure obligations]`.
- **Reporting and investigation:** Staff and stakeholders may report suspected violations to `[Placeholder — designated Ethics/Compliance contact; see Open Questions #13]` without fear of reprisal, including anonymously. All reports receive an initial determination within `[Placeholder — e.g., 5]` business days; investigations are documented, and a decision is communicated to the reporting party (where not anonymous) within `[Placeholder — e.g., 30 calendar days]`.
- All staff, contractors, and applicable other stakeholders (e.g., board members, volunteers) receive education on the Code of Ethical Conduct at orientation and at least annually.

##### 7. Technology Governance and Use of Artificial/Augmented Intelligence (AI)
- **Status: Not currently applicable.** As of the effective date, Upstar does not use AI tools in service delivery, clinical documentation, or data analysis. Standard 1.A.5 requires a written AI governance policy only where AI is in use.
- Should Upstar adopt any AI tool, this section must be completed and approved by the CEO **before** implementation, addressing: (a) how AI is used; (b) disclosure to persons served; (c) protection of health-information privacy; (d) protection of sensitive data; (e) human oversight and accountability; and (f) incident reporting — reviewed at least annually. This manual shall be updated immediately if AI status changes (see Open Questions #14).

##### 8. Leadership Resources, Education, and Accountability
- Leadership provides resources and education enabling staff to understand and apply this manual, including orientation and annual refreshers (1.A.8).
- To demonstrate accountability (1.A.9), the CEO ensures performance information (Policies 1.L–1.M), financial results (Policy 1.F), and risk/safety findings (Policies 1.G–1.H) are reviewed at defined intervals and used in organizational decision-making.

#### Required Documentation
- Current organizational chart; leadership job descriptions; written person-centered philosophy; Policy Review Log; written succession plan; Code of Ethical Conduct and corporate-compliance plan; ethics/compliance report log and investigation records; orientation and annual-training rosters; (if applicable) AI governance policy.

#### Monitoring / KPIs
- 100% of staff/contractors complete person-centered philosophy and ethics training at orientation and annually.
- Organizational chart updated within 7 calendar days of any leadership change.
- All policies reviewed at least annually (0 policies overdue).
- 100% of ethics/compliance reports receive an initial determination within the defined business-day window.

#### Responsible Party
CEO, in conjunction with the leadership team (Clinical Director and designees as noted).

#### Items Requiring Organization Input
1. Communication channels for the person-centered philosophy; 2. Custodian of job descriptions; 3. Quarterly review target months; 4. Designated Ethics/Compliance contact and reporting timeframes; 5. Confirmation of corporate-compliance/self-disclosure obligations with legal counsel; 6. Confirmation AI status remains "none."

### Policy 1.B — Governance

> **Applicability note:** CARF Section 1.B (Governance) applies to organizations with a formal governing board and is optional otherwise. This policy is included for completeness. Upstar shall confirm whether it operates under a formal board or a leadership-only model (see Open Questions #3). Where Upstar has no governing board, Sections 1–7 below are documented as **Not Applicable** and the governance functions are absorbed by the CEO under Policy 1.A.

#### Policy Statement
Where Upstar maintains a governing board, the board implements written governance policies addressing its structure, authority, fiduciary duties, conflicts of interest, executive selection, executive evaluation and compensation, and periodic self-assessment of its own performance. The board maintains a defined relationship with executive leadership and reviews its governance practices at least annually.

**CARF Reference:** Standards 1.B.1–1.B.7 (2026 Behavioral Health Standards Manual, Section 1.B, Governance — optional).

#### Procedure

##### 1. Governance Framework
- The board implements written governance policies defining board structure, size, terms, officer roles, and decision-making authority versus authority delegated to the CEO.

##### 2. Governance Policy Content
- Governance policies address, at minimum: board member roles and responsibilities; fiduciary and legal duties; conflict-of-interest disclosure and management; orientation and ongoing education of board members; and code of conduct.

##### 3. Board–Executive Relationship
- Governance policies define the board's relationship with executive leadership, including delegation of operational authority to the CEO and the boundaries of board involvement in operations.

##### 4. Board Processes
- Board processes include scheduled meetings, recorded minutes, quorum requirements, and documented decisions.

##### 5. Executive Selection and Succession
- Governance policies address executive selection and succession for the CEO position, coordinated with Policy 1.A, Section 5.

##### 6. Executive Evaluation and Compensation
- Governance policies address executive performance evaluation and compensation review on a defined cycle (at least annually), documented in board records.

##### 7. Governance Self-Review
- The board reviews its own governance practices at least annually and documents resulting improvements.

#### Required Documentation
- Board bylaws/governance policies; conflict-of-interest disclosures; board meeting minutes; executive evaluation and compensation records; annual governance self-review.

#### Monitoring / KPIs
- Annual board self-review completed; annual CEO evaluation completed; 100% of board members with current conflict-of-interest disclosures on file.

#### Responsible Party
Governing board (if any); otherwise CEO documents Not-Applicable status.

#### Items Requiring Organization Input
1. Confirm whether Upstar has a formal governing board or operates leadership-only; 2. If board exists, attach bylaws and conflict-of-interest policy; 3. If no board, document Not-Applicable determination and approval authority mapping.

### Policy 1.C — Strategic Planning

#### Policy Statement
Upstar engages in ongoing strategic planning that accounts for the expectations of persons served and other stakeholders; the competitive and regulatory environment; financial opportunities and threats; the organization's capabilities; social determinants of health; service-area demographics; and current and future use of technology and data. Upstar develops, implements, reviews, and updates a written strategic plan at least annually, with documented input from persons served, staff, and other stakeholders, and shares the plan as relevant.

**CARF Reference:** Standards 1.C.1–1.C.3 (2026 Behavioral Health Standards Manual, Section 1.C, Strategic Planning).

#### Procedure

##### 1. Establishing and Maintaining the Strategic Plan
- **Status: Initial plan in development.** Upstar does not yet have a written strategic plan. The CEO shall lead development of the first written strategic plan, to be completed by `[Placeholder — target date; see Open Questions #4]`. Thereafter this section governs ongoing maintenance.
- The CEO leads an annual strategic planning cycle that begins each `[Placeholder — month, e.g., September]` so the updated plan is adopted before the start of the following fiscal/program year.
- Planning gathers documented input from persons served, staff, and other stakeholders through the mechanisms in Policy 1.D.

##### 2. Environmental and Capability Analysis
As part of each cycle, leadership reviews and documents Upstar's position on each factor:
- Expectations of persons served and other stakeholders.
- The competitive environment and the regulatory/legislative environment, including relevant funding or policy changes.
- Financial opportunities and threats.
- Organizational capabilities, including staffing and succession needs in key administrative, financial, and service-delivery positions.
- Social determinants of health affecting persons served (e.g., transportation, housing, food security, healthcare access, exposure to trauma or violence).
- Service-area demographics and how they are changing; relationships with external stakeholders.
- Current and future use of technology and data, including gaps, and how technology will support efficient business processes, effective service delivery, increased access, and performance improvement.
- Findings from the most recent performance analysis (Policy 1.L).

##### 3. Plan Content, Approval, and Sharing
- The written plan identifies organizational priorities, goals, and measurable objectives for the planning period, and assigns responsibility for each.
- The CEO approves the plan, maintains the current version on file, and ensures it is accessible to leadership and, in summary form, to staff and (as relevant) other stakeholders (1.C.3).

##### 4. Annual Review and Update
- The plan is formally reviewed and updated at least annually, documenting progress against prior goals and revising priorities based on updated input and environmental factors. The date and outcome of each review are recorded in the Policy Review Log.

#### Required Documentation
- Written strategic plan; environmental/capability analysis; documented stakeholder input; annual review notes; Policy Review Log entries.

#### Monitoring / KPIs
- Strategic plan reviewed/updated at least annually (on schedule).
- ≥ `[Placeholder — target %]` of strategic objectives on track or completed at annual review.
- Documented input obtained from all three stakeholder groups (persons served, staff, other stakeholders) each cycle.

#### Responsible Party
CEO, in conjunction with the leadership team.

#### Items Requiring Organization Input
1. Target date for the first written strategic plan; 2. Starting month for the annual cycle; 3. Specific input-gathering methods; 4. Target completion threshold for objectives.

### Policy 1.F — Financial Planning and Management

#### Policy Statement
Upstar conducts fiscal management designed to support its mission, outcomes for persons served, and organizational performance objectives. Upstar prepares an annual budget prior to the start of each fiscal year, monitors actual results against budget at least monthly, implements written fiscal policies and internal controls, maintains a disclosed fee structure, obtains an annual independent financial review/audit, and — because it bills third-party payers — conducts at least a quarterly documented review of a representative sample of bills for accuracy.

**CARF Reference:** Standards 1.F.1–1.F.6 (2026 Behavioral Health Standards Manual, Section 1.F, Financial Planning and Management).

#### Procedure

##### 1. Annual Budget Development
- **Status: Formal budget process to be established.** The CEO shall establish a formal written annual budget process beginning with the `[Placeholder — upcoming fiscal year]` budget.
- The CEO prepares a written annual budget prior to the start of each fiscal year (fiscal year = `[Placeholder — e.g., January 1–December 31]`).
- The budget reflects input from staff and stakeholders as appropriate; comparison to the prior year's actuals; anticipated cash-flow needs; and relevant external factors (e.g., reimbursement-rate or regulatory changes).
- The budget documents reasonable projections of revenues, expenses, and capital expenditures, and is approved by the CEO before the fiscal year begins. A provisional budget may be adopted pending final funder approval.
- The approved budget is shared, as appropriate, with staff and other stakeholders.

##### 2. Monitoring Financial Performance
- The CEO reviews actual results against budget at least monthly, identifying and documenting revenues/expenses; internal trends, challenges, opportunities, and management information (e.g., billable vs. non-billable time, payer mix); and external factors.
- Where improvement is needed, the CEO documents and implements corrective actions.
- Financial results are reported, as appropriate, to staff and stakeholders. Overall financial solvency is reviewed at least annually by leadership, with a remediation plan developed if solvency concerns arise.

##### 3. Fiscal Policies and Internal Controls
- Upstar implements written fiscal policies and internal controls establishing responsibility and authority for: purchasing, writing checks, making deposits, petty cash, credit/debit card use, billing, and any investments or fundraising.
- `[Placeholder — document the specific internal control structure: e.g., dual signatures for checks above a set dollar amount, separation of duties between billing and cash handling, check-signing authority; see Open Questions #5]`. Controls are confirmed with Upstar's accountant/bookkeeper to follow generally accepted accounting principles.
- All staff with fiscal responsibilities receive initial training at hire/assignment and ongoing training whenever policies are revised.

##### 4. Billing Accuracy Review (Mandatory — third-party billing)
- **Applicable:** Upstar bills third-party payers (insurance, Medicaid, Medicare), so this review is mandatory under 1.F.6.
- At least quarterly, a trained individual (`[Placeholder — reviewer role; see Open Questions #9]`) reviews a representative sample of bills, comparing dates, units, and types of services billed against documented services actually provided.
- The review documents accuracy, trends, areas needing improvement, and corrective actions.
- Reviews occur **more** frequently than quarterly when billing/coding procedures change, new billing staff are hired, new information systems are implemented, or following corrective training (to confirm effectiveness).

##### 5. Fee Structures

##### 6. Independent Annual Financial Review or Audit
- Upstar obtains an annual independent review or audit of its financial statements by an independent CPA (not the regular bookkeeper/accountant, not a funder representative, not affiliated with leadership).
- The CEO retains the results, including recommendations, and documents management's response, including corrective actions or rationale where none are taken.

#### Monitoring / KPIs

- Budget-vs-actual reviewed monthly (12/12).
- Billing-accuracy review completed at least quarterly; billing accuracy rate ≥ `[Placeholder — e.g., 95%]`.
- Annual independent review/audit completed; all recommendations addressed or rationale documented.

#### Responsible Party
CEO (with bookkeeper/accountant and independent CPA as noted).

#### Items Requiring Organization Input

### Policy 2.F — Promoting Nonviolent Practices (Crisis Prevention & Least Restrictive Intervention)

> **Applicability note:** Standards 2.F.1–2.F.11 apply to any program that uses seclusion or restraint. `[Placeholder — confirm by program whether seclusion/restraint is used; default is a de-escalation-only, least-restrictive model; see Open Questions #10.]` Where seclusion/restraint is not used, Upstar documents that determination; the crisis-prevention and de-escalation elements below still apply as enhancements.

#### Policy Statement
Upstar promotes a culture of nonviolent, trauma-informed crisis prevention that relies on the least restrictive intervention necessary to protect safety. Where seclusion or restraint is used at all, it is used only as a safety intervention of last resort, never for coercion, discipline, convenience, retaliation, or to compensate for inadequate programming or staffing, and is governed by written procedures, training, risk assessment, documentation, order requirements, debriefing, leadership review, and at least annual analysis, consistent with applicable law.

**CARF Reference:** Standards 2.F.1–2.F.11 (2026 Behavioral Health Standards Manual, Section 2.F, Promoting Nonviolent Practices).

#### Procedure

##### 1. Policy Limits on Seclusion/Restraint (2.F.1)
- Policies specify that seclusion or restraint is used only as a last-resort safety intervention; is never used for coercion, discipline, convenience, retaliation, or in lieu of adequate programming/staffing; is administered only by staff competent in the proper techniques; uses no standing orders; and that persons are released immediately upon determination that the risk of harm is no longer present (never held for fixed times or as discipline). All practices comply with federal, Ohio, and other jurisdictional law `[OHIO LEGAL REVIEW REQUIRED]`.

##### 2. Training (2.F.2)
- All staff involved in direct administration of seclusion/restraint receive documented, competency-based training at orientation and at least annually, by qualified trainers, addressing: when seclusion/restraint is indicated; individual and team minimizing-harm interventions; signs of physical distress; physical and psychological risks to persons served and staff; first aid; CPR; and continual assessment for earliest release. Training covers all methods used (physical, mechanical, or chemical restraint).

##### 3. Plan to Eliminate Seclusion/Restraint (2.F.3)
- Upstar implements a plan to eliminate the use of seclusion and/or restraint that includes: the role of leadership; use of data to inform practice; development of a workforce culture supporting resiliency and well-being; input from persons served, families, and advocates; consideration of debriefing results; identification of and actions to minimize environmental factors contributing to unsafe behaviors; specific crisis-prevention strategies; and timelines to reduce use. The plan is shared with staff, persons served, and other stakeholders, reviewed at least annually (progress and improvement areas), and updated as needed.

##### 4. Written Procedures, Risk Assessment, and Documentation (2.F.4–2.F.6)
- Written procedures include protocols for children/youth, adults, and special populations (unless excluded via written scope/admission criteria), and for individual and team interventions (defining team leadership and duties), covering all restraint methods used (2.F.4).
- A risk assessment of each person served addresses medical history, trauma history, history of unsafe behaviors resulting in seclusion/restraint, and interventions previously successful — identifying risks and precautions, and, when applicable, de-escalation actions documented in the record and communicated to staff (2.F.5).
- When seclusion/restraint is used, documentation demonstrates: less-restrictive interventions were attempted first; safe administration considering the person's history; communication to the person that the purpose is safety; monitoring by trained staff per protocol (continual face-to-face when seclusion and restraint are used simultaneously; e.g., checks every 15 minutes of vital signs, circulation, hydration, elimination, distress/agitation, mental status, skin integrity, etc.); ongoing reevaluation of need; removal as soon as the threat ends; immediate medical attention for any injury; and notification as soon as possible to the family and treating practitioner (2.F.6).

##### 5. Orders and Physical Environment (2.F.7–2.F.8)
- Written order procedures specify that orders are given by a physician or advanced-practice professional trained/competent in managing unsafe behaviors, in response to current behavior (no standing or PRN orders); a face-to-face evaluation occurs within one hour of the order; orders do not exceed one hour (child/youth) or four hours (adult); renewals follow face-to-face evaluation, up to a total of 24 hours, after which a new order and face-to-face evaluation are required; orders are entered in the record as soon as possible and not more than two hours after implementation; and the practitioner signs all orders within the time mandated by law (2.F.7) `[OHIO LEGAL REVIEW REQUIRED]`.
- Any room designated for seclusion/restraint provides for safety, continuous face-to-face observation, bathroom access, privacy and dignity, and an identified emergency-exit procedure (2.F.8).

##### 6. Debriefing, Leadership Review, and Annual Analysis (2.F.9–2.F.11)
- A debriefing process is initiated as soon as possible and no more than 24 hours after the incident, including (unless contraindicated) the person served and all involved staff, and documents the incident, contributing factors, and prevention strategies (2.F.9).
- A written procedure provides for leadership review of every use of seclusion/restraint after each occurrence, within a designated timeframe, to determine adherence to policy and the need for performance improvement (2.F.10).
- A documented analysis of the program's use of seclusion/restraint is conducted at least annually, addressing trends (patterns of use, history of use by staff, environmental and program-design contributing factors) and areas needing performance improvement (2.F.11).

#### Required Documentation
- Seclusion/restraint policies and procedures; training records; elimination plan and annual review; per-person risk assessments; per-incident documentation, orders, and debriefings; leadership-review records; annual aggregate analysis.

#### Monitoring / KPIs
- Rate and duration of seclusion/restraint episodes trended toward reduction.
- 100% of episodes with required documentation, debriefing within 24 hours, and leadership review within the defined timeframe.
- Annual aggregate analysis completed.

#### Responsible Party
Clinical Director, with the prescribing/advanced-practice professional for orders/evaluations and leadership for reviews/analysis.

#### Items Requiring Organization Input
1. Whether seclusion/restraint is used, by program; 2. Methods used (physical/mechanical/chemical); 3. Trainer/curriculum; 4. Leadership-review timeframe; 5. Ohio legal confirmation of order/time-limit requirements.

### Policy 2.H — Quality Records Management

Upstar implements systems and procedures for ongoing monitoring of the quality, appropriateness, and utilization of services, accomplished largely through systematic review of the records of persons served. Reviews are conducted at least quarterly by trained, qualified, and independent reviewers on a representative sample of current and closed records, and findings are reported and used for training, performance improvement, and regulatory/contractual and CARF conformance.

**CARF Reference:** Standards 2.H.1–2.H.4 (2026 Behavioral Health Standards Manual, Section 2.H, Quality Records Management).

#### Procedure

##### 1. Documented Records Review (2.H.1)
- Each program conducts a documented review of services at least quarterly that addresses, as evidenced by the record of the person served: quality of service delivery; appropriateness of services; patterns of service utilization; and model fidelity when an evidence-based practice is identified. Reviews are conducted by trained, qualified personnel on a representative sample of records including current and closed records. (Small/short-stay programs may use ongoing supervision/case review, quarterly peer review, or an outside reviewer; long-stay programs may review closed records annually.)

##### 2. Reviewer Independence (2.H.2)
- For records selected for review, the persons responsible for providing the service/treatment are not solely responsible for selecting the records reviewed and are not reviewers of their own records.

##### 3. Systematic, Priority-Based Review (2.H.3)
- Upstar implements a systematic review based on priority areas identified by the program (informed by past results, external audits, or contractual emphasis), considering regulatory/contractual requirements, and ensuring documentation is completed per Upstar policy (including timeliness). Priority areas may include appropriate orientation and informed choice; lawful release of confidential information; thorough/timely assessments; risk factors addressed with safety plans; goals/objectives based on assessment and person input; services related to plan goals at appropriate levels/duration; and completion of transition plans and discharge summaries.

##### 4. Use of Findings (2.H.4)
- Information collected is reported to personnel and used to identify training needs; improve service quality through performance-improvement activities; ensure regulatory/contractual compliance; and improve conformance to CARF standards. Evidence of improvement may include training, policy revisions, or new processes/protocols, correlated with the performance-improvement system (Policy 1.M).

#### Required Documentation
- Quarterly records-review reports; sampling methodology; reviewer assignments; findings reports and resulting training/PI actions.

#### Monitoring / KPIs
- Records review completed at least quarterly per program (4/4).
- Representative sample includes current and closed records.
- Identified training/PI actions tracked to completion.

#### Responsible Party
Clinical Director (or designated quality reviewer); program leadership for oversight.

#### Items Requiring Organization Input
1. Representative-sample methodology/size; 2. Reviewer designation and independence arrangement; 3. Priority review areas per program.

### Policy 2.I — Service Delivery Using Information and Communication Technologies (Telehealth)

> **Applicability note:** Standards 2.I.1–2.I.7 apply where a program uses information and communication technologies (ICT) to deliver services (e.g., telehealth, telemental health, virtual services, remote monitoring). Strictly informational use (e.g., a website, reminders, scheduling) or self-directed apps are not ICT service delivery. `[Placeholder — confirm which programs use ICT and the approved platform list; see Open Questions #8.]`

#### Policy Statement
Where Upstar delivers services via ICT, the quality and integrity of services are comparable to in-person services. Upstar implements written procedures addressing consent, recording, modality decision-making, technology readiness, identity/location verification, privacy, and technology-disruption response; trains staff and educates persons served/families on equipment and environment; provides contact/technical-assistance mechanisms; verifies session participants and information; maintains equipment; and implements ICT-specific emergency procedures.

**CARF Reference:** Standards 2.I.1–2.I.7 (2026 Behavioral Health Standards Manual, Section 2.I, Service Delivery Using Information and Communication Technologies).

#### Procedure

##### 1. Written ICT Procedures (2.I.1)
- Written procedures address: consent of the person served to participate in ICT service delivery; audio/video recording and photographing the person served; decision-making about ICT versus in-person services at the beginning of and throughout services; confirmation that necessary technology/equipment is available and functions prior to and as needed during service delivery at both the person's and provider's locations; verification at **each** encounter of the identity of the person served, the identity of the provider, and the physical location of the person served; maintaining privacy during service delivery; and responding to technology disruption (including a back-up communication method).

##### 2. Staff Training (2.I.2)
- As appropriate, staff who deliver services via ICT receive documented, competency-based training on: delivering services effectively via ICT (human factors; crisis-response procedures; assessment of risk factors in the person's environment; modifying treatment techniques/interventions for virtual delivery); and the equipment used (features, setup, use, maintenance, safety considerations, infection control, troubleshooting).

##### 3. Person/Family Instruction (2.I.3)
- As appropriate, instruction and training are provided to persons served, family/support-system members, and others on the equipment used (features, setup, use, maintenance, safety, infection control, troubleshooting) and on creating an appropriate environment to receive services (safe, private, connected, on time, prepared).

##### 4. Access, Support, and Modifications (2.I.4)
- ICT service delivery includes: information to persons served on how to contact the program and the expected response timeframe; staff to provide technical assistance accessing services; staff to address service-delivery questions; and, based on identified need, an appropriate facilitator at the person's location and modifications to treatment techniques/interventions, equipment, materials, and the environment (accessibility, privacy, usability).

##### 5. Pre-Session Verification and Information (2.I.5)
- Prior to the start of each session, any participants in addition to the person served are identified, and the program provides information relevant to the session (e.g., provider credentials, structure/timing, recordkeeping, scheduling, between-session contact, privacy/security, potential risks, safety, confidentiality, billing, rights/responsibilities). For ongoing (non-session) ICT, relevant information is reviewed at a minimum frequency (e.g., at least annually) or based on need.

##### 6. Equipment Maintenance and Emergency Procedures (2.I.6–2.I.7)
- Upstar maintains equipment in accordance with manufacturers' recommendations.
- Emergency procedures address the unique aspects of ICT service delivery, including: identification of an emergency contact for the person served (with phone number); identification of local emergency resources (with phone numbers); and the provider becoming familiar with the emergency procedures at the person's location, if such procedures exist.

#### Required Documentation
- Written ICT procedures; ICT consent records; staff ICT-training records; person/family instruction records; approved-platform list; equipment-maintenance logs; ICT emergency-contact and local-resource information per person served.

#### Monitoring / KPIs
- 100% of ICT encounters with verified identity (person and provider) and physical location of the person served.
- 100% of ICT persons served with documented emergency contact and local emergency resources.
- Staff delivering ICT services trained and competency-documented.

#### Responsible Party
Clinical Director, with IT support for platforms/equipment.

#### Items Requiring Organization Input
1. Which programs use ICT and the approved platform list; 2. Audio-only eligibility boundaries `[OHIO LEGAL REVIEW REQUIRED — payer/telehealth rules]`; 3. ICT consent form; 4. Back-up communication method.

### Appendix D — Key Definitions / Glossary

- **Person served** — an individual receiving services from Upstar Mental Health Services.
- **Staff** — Upstar employees (full-time, part-time, per diem) involved in delivery, oversight, or support of services.
- **Contractor** — an individual or organization delivering services or supports to Upstar under contract rather than as an employee.
- **Leadership** — the CEO and the leadership team (e.g., Clinical Director, designated officers) accountable for organizational functions.
- **CEO** — Chief Executive Officer; the approval authority for this manual and Upstar's senior executive.
- **Person-centered philosophy** — Upstar's documented commitment to individuality, autonomy, respect for diversity, cultural competence, empowerment, partnership, valued life roles, and quality of life.
- **Critical incident** — an event requiring reporting and review, including (without limitation) medication errors, seclusion/restraint, injury, communicable disease, aggression/violence, weapons, elopement/wandering, vehicular/biohazard accidents, abuse, neglect, suicide/attempted suicide, sexual assault, and overdose.
- **Seclusion / restraint** — safety interventions of last resort (physical, mechanical, or chemical) used only to prevent harm; governed by Policy 2.F.
- **ICT (information and communication technologies)** — technologies enabling service delivery when the person served and provider are not in the same physical location (e.g., telehealth); governed by Policy 2.I.
- **Person-centered plan** — the individualized service plan based on assessment, expressing the goals, objectives, services, responsible parties, and timeframes for a person served.
- **Interpretive summary** — the written integration and interpretation of assessment information that informs person-centered planning.
- **Representative sample** — a subset of records selected so that review findings can be reasonably generalized, including current and closed records.
- **KPI** — key performance indicator; a measurable value used to monitor performance against a target.
- **`[OHIO LEGAL REVIEW REQUIRED]`** — marker indicating an Ohio-specific statutory citation, threshold, or procedure requiring legal-counsel/compliance confirmation before adoption.
- **`[Placeholder — ...]`** — marker indicating an organizational decision or local operational detail Upstar must confirm.
- **`[ENHANCEMENT]`** — marker indicating a value-added control adopted by Upstar exceeding the explicit CARF requirement.
